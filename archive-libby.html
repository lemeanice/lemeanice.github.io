Libby App Redesign

A design thinking project run with a team of 9 classmates. We interviewed 15 users to understand how people actually use the Libby ebook app and found that the biggest friction points weren't the obvious ones.

Context
Libby is a great product with some rough edges

Libby is a library ebook app: free, well-designed, and genuinely useful. But users had real pain points that weren't well-documented publicly. Our brief was to identify unmet needs and prototype a solution using design thinking methodology.

We chose Libby because several of us used it regularly and had quiet frustrations with it. Starting with personal experience meant we had a hypothesis to test, and our user interviews mostly confirmed it, while adding texture we hadn't expected.

Research
15 user interviews and a few surprises

We ran 15 semi-structured interviews with Libby users across different reading habits: casual readers, power users, people who had tried the app and stopped using it. The goal was to understand their borrowing and discovery patterns, not just their UI complaints.

The biggest finding: users didn't struggle with the reading experience. They struggled with the wait. Libby operates on library hold queues where popular books often have weeks-long waits. Users weren't sure whether to wait, borrow something else, or abandon the queue entirely. The decision logic wasn't supported by the app.

A secondary finding: discovery was weak. Users wanted to find books based on mood or vibe, not just category. The search and browse experience was functional but not inspiring.

What we designed
Two focused improvements to the borrow and discovery flow
Hold queue experience
Smarter hold management
Redesigned the holds page to give users better information about their queue position and estimated wait time. Added a "while you wait" recommendation that surfaces books available immediately with a similar vibe.
Discovery
Mood-based browsing
A new entry point for browsing by reading mood ("cozy", "intense", "quick read" etc.) rather than just genre. Mapped to Libby's existing catalogue taxonomy but surfaced in a more intuitive way.
Outreach
We actually reached out to OverDrive (Libby's maker)

At the end of the project, we put together a summary of our findings and prototype and reached out to OverDrive (the company behind Libby) to share what we'd found. We didn't get a reply, but it felt like the right thing to do. If you've done user research that could genuinely improve a product, it's worth trying to close the loop.

This was one of those uni projects where the learning felt real. Running 15 user interviews, synthesising across a team of 9, and building a prototype from scratch, it was closer to actual product work than most coursework gets.
